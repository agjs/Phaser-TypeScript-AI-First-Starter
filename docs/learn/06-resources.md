# 06 — Resources

Curated, not dumped. Every entry here is something worth your time. If it's not on this list and someone swears by it, it's probably still worth a look — but start here.

## Books (read in roughly this order)

1. **[The Art of Game Design: A Book of Lenses](https://www.amazon.com/Art-Game-Design-Book-Lenses/dp/1138632058)** — Jesse Schell.
   The single best book on game design for a programmer. Each chapter is a "lens" — a question to ask your game. Skim all 100 lenses; deep-read the ones your game cries for. Referenced in every game-design conversation worth having.
2. **[A Theory of Fun for Game Design](https://www.raphkoster.com/games/a-theory-of-fun-for-game-design/)** — Raph Koster.
   Short, illustrated. Explains *why* games are fun, which matters because you'll spend a lot of hours asking yourself that question.
3. **[Game Feel](http://www.game-feel.com/)** — Steve Swink.
   Exactly what the title says. Dense. Worth the read once you have something moving on screen.
4. **[Rules of Play](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262240451/rules-of-play/)** — Salen & Zimmerman.
   Heavy and academic. Reference material. Skim chapters as you hit specific problems.

Optional:
- **[Level Up! The Guide to Great Video Game Design](https://www.amazon.com/Level-Up-Guide-Great-Design/dp/1118877160)** — Scott Rogers. Lighter, practical.
- **[The Design of Everyday Things](https://www.nngroup.com/books/design-everyday-things-revised/)** — Don Norman. Not game-specific but every game has UI.

## YouTube channels (highest signal first)

- **[Game Maker's Toolkit](https://www.youtube.com/@GMTK)** — Mark Brown. Best game-design channel alive. Essays on specific games and systems. Watch "What Makes a Good Combat System?", "The Rise of the Systemic Game", anything on Celeste or Hollow Knight.
- **[Masahiro Sakurai on Creating Games](https://www.youtube.com/@sora_sakurai_en)** — Smash Bros director, 2-5 minute videos on concrete design lessons.
- **[Jonas Tyroller](https://www.youtube.com/@JonasTyroller)** — solo dev devlogs with deep design reasoning. Watch "When to Stop Making Games."
- **[Thomas Brush](https://www.youtube.com/@ThomasBrush)** — more production-focused, interviews with other solo devs.
- **[Brackeys (archive)](https://www.youtube.com/@Brackeys)** — tutorials mostly Unity but concepts transfer.
- **[Sebastian Lague](https://www.youtube.com/@SebastianLague)** — procedural generation and graphics deep dives. Watch "Coding Adventure" series.
- **[Extra Credits](https://www.youtube.com/@extracredits)** — accessible design history.

Watch one video per week, minimum. It stays with you.

## Learn by playing

Play with intent. When you enjoy something, stop and ask: *what specifically is working here?*

- **Celeste** — pixel-perfect platforming. Study the dash and the checkpoint system.
- **Into the Breach** — every action deterministic. Study constrained turn-based design.
- **Vampire Survivors** — core loop reduced to its atomic form. Study the auto-attack.
- **Slay the Spire** — deck-building loop. Study card synergies + relic design.
- **Tetris** — the platonic game. Study why it's still fresh 40 years later.
- **Hollow Knight** — metroidvania done right. Study the world design.
- **A Short Hike** — small, complete, joyful. Study the scope.
- **Baba Is You** — rules as gameplay. Study the elegance.
- **Stephen's Sausage Roll** — if you want your brain to hurt. Study puzzle design.

## Reference material on specific topics

- **[Red Blob Games](https://www.redblobgames.com/)** — the best programmer-facing site on grids, pathfinding, hex maps, procedural generation. Bookmark it.
- **[GameProgrammingPatterns.com](https://gameprogrammingpatterns.com/)** — Robert Nystrom's book, free online. Design patterns specifically for games.
- **[easings.net](https://easings.net/)** — every tween easing curve, interactive.
- **[lospec.com/palette-list](https://lospec.com/palette-list)** — pixel art palettes. Pick one. Use only those colors. Your game will look coherent.

## Asset stores (when AI isn't enough)

- **[itch.io asset packs](https://itch.io/game-assets)** — huge range, indie-friendly licenses, pay-what-you-want.
- **[OpenGameArt](https://opengameart.org/)** — free, CC-licensed. Old but useful.

- **[Unity Asset Store](https://assetstore.unity.com/)** — most assets work as source material for other engines too (check license).

## Game jams (highly recommended)

Participating in a jam is the fastest accelerator for solo dev skills. You ship in 48 hours, compared to months.

- **[Global Game Jam](https://globalgamejam.org/)** — annual, January, worldwide.
- **[Ludum Dare](https://ldjam.com/)** — quarterly, competitive and cooperative tracks.
- **[itch.io jams](https://itch.io/jams)** — hundreds per month. Pick any themed one that matches your idea.
- **[GMTK Game Jam](https://itch.io/jam/gmtk-jam)** — Mark Brown's annual jam, massive participation.

## What to do right after finishing this doc

1. Watch "The Art of Screenshake" (10 min).
2. Sketch your game's core loop in six verbs (5 min).
3. Join one community from the list above (5 min).
4. Commit to participating in one game jam in the next 3 months (it's free).
5. Start building.

The resources will be here when you need them. Go make the thing.
